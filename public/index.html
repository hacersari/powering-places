Exploring Energy Consumptions Across America

Team Members: Anju Lam,Aynur Sari,Charyl Li,Masaru Chida

Welcome to our exploration of energy consumption patterns across the United States. Energy use is a fundamental aspect of our daily lives, impacting our economy, environment, and individual households. Understanding these patterns is crucial for making informed decisions about energy policies, conservation efforts, and our future energy needs.

Residential Electricity Consumption Heatmap by County in the U.S


First, we have a choropleth map that shows the chosen energy consumption by each county in the United States. We included a drop down menu to choose between seven energy types, to see if there are any similarities or differences in consumption across different energy types. Patterns we will to look into are which counties have the highest natural gas consumption, and whether this also correlates to a high consumption in other energy types like diesel and gasoline. We will also look into patterns between residential, commercial, and industrial energy consumption.

We also included a scale slider since the counties can get pretty small in a zoomed out screen, so you could zoom into see details about smaller counties.

Comparing Electricity and Natural Gas Consumption & Expenditure


Delving deeper, we compare electricity and natural gas consumption and expenditure through a pair of line graphs. First, we can observe an obvious positive relationship between energy consumption and cost. A more interesting observation though is that there is a much higher consumption of natural gas in the United States. Although the units are different which makes it hard for a direct comparison, 1MWh of electricity consumption is roughly equivalent to the energy content of 3.412 MCf of natural gas, so the observation still holds that we generally consume more natural gas as an energy source. Another observation is that we generally spend less money on natural gas than electricity. This could lead is to the fact that natural gas tends to be cheaper than electricity for many applications, primarily due to differences in production, distribution, and efficiency factors. In general, natural gas has lower production costs as it is cheaper to produce compared to electricity that has to be generated from other sources such as coal or renewable sources. Additionally, natural gas pipelines are widespread and well-established in many regions, allowing for efficient transportation and distribution of gas to users (keeping the distribution costs relatively low).

U.S. Residential Electricity Consumption by Per Capita Level


We also consider energy consumption on a per capita basis, which sheds light on individual consumption behaviors and regional efficiency. By analyzing per capita consumption, we can identify which counties consume more energy relative to their population, offering a nuanced understanding of energy efficiency and sustainability across regions.


Our exploration then extends to the employment landscape of the U.S. We present a map that colorfully visualizes the average employment rates by state, offering a comparative overview of the nation's employment scene. Understanding the relationship between employment rates and energy consumption is key. Areas with higher employment may see increased energy use due to industrial and commercial activity. However, they may also have more resources for investing in energy efficiency and renewable resources, potentially lowering the overall energy footprint.

Population and Consumption Correlations


We further examine the correlation between population size and residential energy consumption. Through scatter plots, we explore whether larger populations lead to proportionally higher energy use or if there are efficiencies of scale. These correlations can inform how we approach urban planning and energy management in densely populated areas versus more rural settings.

State-by-State Greenhouse Gas Emissions by Energy Source


In our project, we also took into account greenhouse gas emissions, which are closely intertwined with energy production and consumption. This connection arises from the burning of fossil fuels, releasing carbon dioxide and other greenhouse gases like methane and nitrous oxide into the atmosphere.
Displayed above is a visualization depicting greenhouse gas emissions across various states, categorized by the energy sources responsible for these emissions. This visualization allows us to compare and analyze the states with the highest greenhouse gas emissions. Additionally, we can delve deeper into individual states to examine the distribution and proportion of energy sources contributing to these emissions.
